I keep starting this and deleting it.

Not because I don't know what to say. Because I do. And it sounds mental when I type it out.

A safety company's AI got used in an airstrike the same day it was banned for being too safe. That happened. That's real. I didn't make that up. Nobody made that up. It's in the timeline with sources.

And nobody seems to be losing their mind about it.

That's the bit I can't get past.

You think I'm some kind of conscientious objector. I'm not. You think I'm taking some moral high ground. I'm not. I downloaded the large language models. I marvelled at the convenience of access to the most powerful network of compute I could ever have imagined. My generation lived for 20 years before Google was invented, so to have that power no more than 2 feet from our fingertips 24 hours a day is near science fiction. But it's clear to me that at no point can this race have been about slightly speeding up Googling whatever random thought, question, fact, or date pops into our heads. That makes no sense. Look how the words "100 billion dollars" has become a perfectly normal thing to hear. It's not normal. Now tens of trillions is heard on a daily basis. Nobody thinks about that number for even a second. It's incomprehensible. That's not the cost of a fast Google search in your pocket. It was never for that.

Not the technology. The technology does what technology does. It gets built. It gets bigger. Someone finds a use for it that wasn't in the brochure. This has happened with every powerful thing humans have ever made. Splitting the atom was going to give us free energy. It gave us Hiroshima first.

What gets me is the bit where everyone involved knew. Not suspected. Knew. Said it out loud. Published papers about it. Quit their jobs over it. Went on podcasts about it. And then it happened anyway. Exactly how they said it would.

Hinton quit Google and said he regretted his life's work. Then nothing changed. Altman told Congress he was worried. Then went back and made it more powerful. Amodei drew red lines. Then his AI was in a war zone.

These aren't stupid people. These aren't bad people. That's the whole problem. If they were stupid or bad you could point at them and say there's your villain. But they're not. They're smart and they're scared and they're building it anyway because if they don't, someone else will. And that someone else might be less careful.

That's the logic. That's always the logic. It was the logic with nuclear weapons. It was the logic with chemical weapons. It's the logic now.

And it's not wrong. That's the worst part. It's not wrong and it leads somewhere terrible and knowing that doesn't help.

The question that needs asking is who has their finger on the button. Or maybe it's how many buttons are there. Maybe the answer is we all have one. Or the scary answer might be that we have just handed it to a non-human entity for the first time in history. And I don't know what that means.

I don't have an answer. I'm not pretending to. I made a website. It's got a silly Terminator theme and a bunch of pointless things that I hope make it interesting and fun to read and explore. I added a game. Yes because I could, but also because I feel it tells the real story that you have seen and read right here. That has to mean something.

I hope I'm not alone in grasping the gravity of the current climate. COMPETITION DISTORTION  ·  HERO / VILLAIN INSTABILITY  ·  SYSTEM PRESSURE
EXPAND
PRIMARY ANALYSIS

There is a tendency to treat “hero” and “villain” as fixed categories. They are not. The distinction is clear at the extremes, but unstable at the centre.

This is not a justification for wrongdoing. It is an observation of proximity.

There exists a point where “good” becomes “bad,” where “right” becomes “wrong.” That point is not cleanly defined. It is influenced, shaped, and in some cases erased by the conditions surrounding the decision.

SHARED TRAIT: THE EXTRAORDINARY

A hero is defined as someone willing to do what the average person would not.

That definition applies equally to a villain.

Both operate outside the norm. Both take actions others avoid. The separation is not in capability or behaviour, but in how those actions are later classified.

MOTIVE DOES NOT RESOLVE THE LINE

Consider the act of building a company capable of changing the world.

To attempt it, you must already exist within the “extraordinary” class. The typical motivations—money, power, recognition—are not treated as moral failings. They are treated as indicators of success.

We do not measure why these outcomes are pursued. We measure whether they are achieved.

If motive does not define the boundary, it cannot stabilise it.

OUTCOME IS NOT STABLE

If the distinction cannot be found in motive, it is often assigned based on outcome.

But outcomes are inconsistent, delayed, and frequently reinterpreted. The same action can be framed as necessary or reckless depending on when it is evaluated and by whom.

This does not produce clarity. It produces narrative.

COMPETITION AS THE DISTORTING FORCE

The instability exists because of competition.

Competition introduces pressure. Pressure introduces compromise. Over time, the line does not move—it degrades.

The system rewards those who act decisively within that pressure. It does not reward those who hesitate to preserve the boundary.

Under sustained competition, the distinction between hero and villain is not crossed in a single step. It is eroded, incrementally, until it is no longer visible.

Competition does not merely reward decisive action. It progressively corrupts the standard used to judge it.
